ericpdollard.com                 emediapress.com  Three Phase Systems  
 
Three phase is by far the most common polyphase system. The method of 
symmetrical components for three phase has become a foundation  in 
power engineering and its use is wide -spread. The theoretical 
understanding is vague, however, and has never been clearly presented. 
This has limited t he application of the method to situations of common 
occurrence, rendering details unseen.  
 
Three phase is a most basic set of sequences, a simple forward and reverse 
for the most part. This aspect recommends it as a starting point for the 
development of a special theory. Textbooks available on three phase 
symmetrical components are readily availabl e or reference on this subject.  
 
Four phase will follow in the development of its special theory, and this 
will resemble the fundamental Four Quadrant Theory of  Electricity. The 
quadrapolar theory is further developed in this four phase theory. Of 
particular interest in four phase is the appearance of a new sequence, the 
Alternate  Sequence .  
 
Two phase will next be developed, this reducing the theory of phases to  its 
arithmetic form. Two phase also provides insight to the process of the 
alternating sequence found in even numbered systems of phases,  
 
2,  4,  6,  8,  etc. 
 
Finally one phase is considered. It completes the Pythagorean Nomen 
Matrix. The only Sequence  here is zero sequence, or no sequence at all. This 
will take the theory into another realm to be considered in latter chapters.

ericpdollard.com                 emediapress.com  (2)  Polyphase systems in practice are often not balanced systems. The 
balance becomes distorted by harmonics, fault conditions, and large 
alternating (вҖңsingle phaseвҖқ) loads. The practice of ba sing calculations on 
the subdivision of the polyphase system into N alternating systems fails in 
a condition of imbalance. Therefore, an unbalanced three phase system 
cannot be given as three вҖңsingle phaseвҖқ systems.  
 
The study of unbalanced systems began with Ernst Alexanderson and the 
analysis of the unbalance caused by large electric railroad loads. This was 
advanced by L. G. Stokvis and work progressed to the efforts of Dr. 
Charles Fortescue. It was discovered analytically that any three phase 
system ca n be resolved into three distinct electric waves, a forward rotating 
wave, a backwards rotating wave, and a non -rotating unidirectional wave. 
The non -rotating wave was the most difficult to arrive at analytically. Also, 
it is this wave that causes the most  trouble in the practical systems. It is 
known as the zero sequence wave, and it represents an asymmetrical 
imbalance.  
 
These three component waves are:  
 
0)  Zero Sequence, Non Rotating  
 
1)  Positive Sequence, Forward Rotating  
 
2)  Negative Sequence, Reve rse Rotating  
 
In each component wave all three phases are of Equal  Magnitude , thus one 
phase defines the other two. As a result вҖңSingle PhaseвҖқ analysis is possible 
for each sequence. This is known as the Method of Symmetrical 
Components. The composite of t he three waves combined engenders the 
general unbalanced wave. The Fortescue Method involves the translation 
between the unbalanced system and its sequence components.
